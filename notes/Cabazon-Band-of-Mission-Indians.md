# The Cabazon Band of Mission Indians

The [Cabazon Band of Mission Indians](https://cabazontribe.com/) is, according to [Wkipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabazon_Band_of_Mission_Indians), a federally recognized tribe of Cahuilla Indians located in Riverside County, California.

Quoting from (Rossum, 2011) the "Cabazon Band was the first Native America tribe in the United States to have gaming on its reservation." Since 1991, they run the [Fantasy Springs Resort Casino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fantasy_Springs_Resort_Casino). 

They won the right to run tribal gaming facilities in the 1987 Supreme Court decision [California v. Cabazon Band of Mission Indians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_v._Cabazon_Band_of_Mission_Indians), ruling that states are barred from interfering with tribal gaming.

The legal situation is interesting. Without going into details, previous courts had made the distinction between civi/regulatory and criminal/prohibitory. States are allowed to enforce criminal/prohibitory regulations. So why did gambling fall one way and not the other way?

The consequences of the decision were significant, both with respect to tribal sovereignty and the economy of the tribes. According to (Belanger etal, 2016) "In the United States, Indian gaming is a $29.9 billion industry, with some 240 American Indian tribes operating over 470 gaming operations in 28 states."

In 1988, Congress enacted the [Indian Gaming Regulatory Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Gaming_Regulatory_Act) (IGRA). "The stated purposes of the act include providing a legislative basis for the operation/regulation of Indian gaming, protecting gaming as a means of generating revenue for the tribes, encouraging economic development of these tribes, and protecting the enterprises from negative influences (such as organized crime)." Both states and tribes are required to negotiate the about specific rules and regulations.

## Historical Remarks

Rossum's book contains detailed information about the legal history of American Indians. I can't repeat this here and just collected some links on the topics discussed in the book (and would recommend the book to those who are interested).

[American Indians And The Constitution](https://www.encyclopedia.com/politics/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/american-indians-and-constitution).

[Marshall Trilogy](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/crsj/publications/human_rights_magazine_home/2014_vol_40/vol--40--no--1--tribal-sovereignty/short_history_of_indian_law/). Three Supreme Court Cases (1823-1832), primarily authored by Chief Justice John Marshall, established federal primacy in Indian affairs, excluded state law from Indian country, and recognized tribal governance authority. Moreover, these cases established the place of Indian nations in the American dual sovereign structure that still governs today. Marshall also held that, as a trustee, the federal government is obliged to act in the best interests of the tribes.

[Dawes Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dawes_Act). Aka Allotment Act (1887-1934) made American Indians private owners of their land and resulted in the tribes loosing control of about 100 million acres of land or about two-thirds of the land base they held in 1887.

[Public Law 280](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_Law_280). 1953. The Act mandated a transfer of federal law enforcement authority within certain tribal nations to state governments in six states: California, Minnesota, Nebraska, Oregon, Wisconsin, and Alaska.

[Indian Civil Rights Act](https://study.com/academy/lesson/the-indian-civil-rights-act-of-1968.html). 1968. The Indian Civil Rights Act of 1968 granted Native Americans, for the first time, full access to the United States Bill of Rights.

Tribal Self-Governance Act 1994. Congress recognized the inherent sovereignty of the tribes, permanently established the tribal self-governance as the policy of the federal government, and stated that it was the goal of the US to negotiate with tribes on a government-to-government basis while retaining the federal trust relationship.

After this background, Rossum discusses in detail how on the background of this history the case *California v. Cabazon Band of Mission Indians* played out.
