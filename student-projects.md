#### Postgraduates

I [directed various PhDs](phd-students.html) in logic, category theory, and computer science. More recently, I am also interested branching out to other fields such as engineering, economics, and the social sciences. The rationale is that these areas use a substantial amount of mathematics stemming from physics (such as calculus, linear algebra, probability and statistics) but much less of the more recent mathematics developed for computer science (this includes not only algorithms and complexity theory but also logic and category theory). This looks like a research opportunity ...

#### Undergraduates

A typical research project in theoretical computer science is to take a research article, study it, learn the math required, and apply it to some interesting problem.

From a technical point of view, for example,

- you could learn how to use an interactive theorem prover (Lean, Isabelle, Coq, Agda, ...) and then implement some proofs on a particular topic of interst;

- instead of interactive theorem provers, there are many other logic-based tools developed in theoretical computer science such as SAT-solvers, SMT-solvers, model checkers, ...

If you are already interested in some area of maths and computer science and want to learn it by doing a research project get in touch. Here are some of the things I'd be interested in:

From a philosophical point of view, for example, Larry Moss has been doing exciting work on extending Aristotle's syllogistic logics. There are fascinating connections to computer science (lambda-calculus, ...), linguistics, AI and machine learning.

From a data science point of view, I am interested in using citation data to draw maps of areas of science in order to help generating new research questions.

Are you a chess player? As an AI project, I would like to develop chess programs that are better at "human chess". How to make chess programs play "more human" without dumbing them down? How to make them suggest moves and lines that are meaningful to human players? Can this be adapted to different human styles?

There is much more, of course, I am happy to talk about any ideas and to learn something new, for example [reversible computing](https://hackmd.io/@alexhkurz/H1PIwnSqw).

#### Big Questions

Big questions are not always easy to turn into concrete research questions, but that only makes them more inetersting.

Logic has taken a quantitative turn. Instead of being concerned only with 2 truth values, there are applications of many-valued logics to pure mathematics, software engineering and control theory, to name just a few. There is still much work to be done ...

Theorem proving has been a very successful application of computer science to mathematics ... but we have not been getting much closer to the point where mathematicians who dislike programming feel comfortable to make use of this technology. Why?

Machine learning is great as long as we have, or can generate, large amounts of data. But humans are capable to learn succesfully from small numbers of examples. One of the ideas to tackle this gap is to bring together symbolic and statistical AI. While there is work along these lines, this is still in its infancy ...

What is a proof? What is an algorithm? What is information?
